Lake Chad Basin Emergency
Now is the time

The Lake Chad Basin is facing one of the largest humanitarian crises in the world.

In 2016, needs have grown in dramatic fashion. Raids and suicide bombings have caused death and destruction, wiping out livelihoods and vital infrastructure. Millions of families struggle with displacement, hunger and malnutrition.

2017 has to be the turning point for the affected people. The response has been scaled up significantly. Governments, UN agencies and NGOs are joining forces to save the lives of the most vulnerable, and lay the groundwork for a better future.

Now is the time to curb the trend in Africa’s most acute crisis.

“ Now is the time for the response to match the plight, and the generosity and resilience, of the people in the Lake Chad region. Now is the time to stand in solidarity with the people of Nigeria, Cameroon, Chad and Niger. “ Under-Secretary-General for Humanitarian Affairs and Emergency Relief Coordinator, Stephen O’Brien Statement to the Security Council on Syria, New York, 28 April 2016

In the harsh environment of the Sahel, the fresh waters of Lake Chad have for centuries been a source of life. Today, the lake has shrunk to one tenth of its size and the people living on its shores suffer one of the world‘s most devastating crises brought about by climate change, abject poverty and violent extremism.

Communities in the region, which is around the size of Ireland and spans north-east Nigeria, Cameroon, Chad and Niger, are among the most resilient in the world. Mostly living in deep poverty and without essential services like electricity or running water, they struggle to adapt to demographic change and environmental degradation.

Over the past years, their highly fragile lives and livelihoods have been shattered. Raids, killings and forced recruitment by Boko Haram militants have caused widespread trauma and emptied entire villages.

Nine million people in the Lake Chad Basin - that is almost one in every two people - need urgent help.

And as Lake Chad’s waters recede, so does the hope of the people living on its shore.
